About Me

My name is Ursula Jongebloed (pronounced "youngblood") and I'm a PhD student in Atmospheric Sciences at the University of Washington working with Becky Alexander. I study atmospheric chemistry and climate in the Arctic. Before coming to UW, I studied chemistry and Earth sciences at Dartmouth College and worked as a federal contractor for the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.

I use ice cores and atmospheric chemistry modeling to understand past sulfate aerosol sources and chemistry during the preindustrial and industrial eras. My Phd Research can be broadly divided into three topics by source of Arctic sulfate aerosols: volcanoes, marine phytoplankton, and human activities

Research

Sulfate aerosols from passive volcanic degassing

One of the most uncertain aspects of climate modeling is the abundance of sulfate aerosols during the preindustrial era. Passive (i.e. non-eruptive) volcanic degassing is a significant and uncertain source of natural sulfate. Most climate models simulate preindustrial and present-day volcanic sulfur emissions based on present-day satellite observations of sulfur dioxide and assume that non-eruptive emissions have been roughly constant since the preindustrial. We investigated this assumption by measuring sulfur isotopes in a Greenland ice core. By comparing our measurements to a global chemical transport model, we found that passive volcanic degassing contributed more to preindustrial Arctic sulfate aerosol than models predict. This result has important implications for Arctic radiative forcing.

Related publications: Jongebloed, et al. (2022), Underestimated passive volcanic sulfur degassing implies overestimated anthropogenic aerosol radiative forcing, Geophysical Research Letters (in revision)



Marine phytoplankton sulfur emissions and chemistry

Marine phytoplankton are primary producers in ocean ecosystems and influence climate through emissions of a sulfur compound called dimethyl sulfide (DMS) to the atmosphere. After emission, DMS is converted to two other sulfur compounds: methanesulfonic acid (MSA) and sulfate. MSA can be measured in ice cores and is used to study past DMS emissions from phytoplankton. However, human-caused changes to atmospheric chemistry affect how much DMS is converted to MSA vs. sulfate; thus, both compounds must be considered when studying past phytoplankton DMS emissions. In this work, we use sulfur isotopes to determine how much ice core sulfate comes from each source (DMS, volcanoes, human activities), allowing us to measure how much sulfate comes from phytoplankton and compare it to MSA. In general, we find that trends in DMS-derived sulfate do not align with trends in MSA, indicating that atmospheric chemistry must be considered when interpreting ice core records of MSA.

Related publications: Jongebloed et al. (2023), Industrial-era decline in Arctic methanesulfonic acid is offset by increased biogenic sulfate aerosol in prep.

Anthropogenic and natural sulfate over industrial era

Over the industrial era (1850 to present day), anthropogenic activities have increased the abundance of sulfate aerosols to the atmosphere. These anthropogenic sulfate aerosols partially offset the warming caused by human greenhouse gases. However, the cooling caused by anthropogenic sulfate is difficult to estimate because the abundance of sulfate from natural sulfate (e.g. volcanoes, marine phytoplankton, and sea salt) is unknown. In this study, we use a Greenland ice core to investigate how much sulfate comes from anthropogenic activities versus natural sources over the industrial era

Related publications: Jongebloed et al. (in prep), Anthropogenic and natural sulfate in a Greenland ice core over the industrial era.
